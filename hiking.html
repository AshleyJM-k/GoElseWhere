Walking and hiking throughout New Zealand is the best way to see beautiful landscapes and explore vast wilderness areas.





The diverse tracks and great walks across New Zealand's North and South Islands national parks and reserves

offers a variety of hiking options to suit all levels of fitness and experience.

This means theres something for everyone to enjoy from leisurely nature trails that bring unique ecosystems into focus,

to challenging multi-day adventures in remote, untouched wilderness.


When your short on time and theres something remarkable to see, you can be sure theres a short walk or day hike for you to

enjoy on your trip to experience New Zealand's unique landscape and scenic views.

Throughout New Zealand there are 10 Great Walks that range between two to ten day adventures.

These Great Walks offer a variety of elevation and difficulty to meet everyones experience levels from easy to advanced.


To experience some of New Zealand's best hikes be sure to check out Tongariro Crossing or Hooker Valley to get

a glimpse of the tallest mountain in New Zealand Mount Cook (Aoraki Mt Cook),

or check out Roy's Peak for an amazing view of Lake Wanaka.

If your looking for an epic challenge check out New Zealand's toughest track, Dusky Track, located in the South Island.

This track offers many challenges including, three-wire bridges, steep climbs, a constant change in weather,

swarms of sandflies, and be prepared to get extremely muddy.


Best Time to go Hiking

If your looking to plan a trip to spend time hiking and exploring the beautiful places New Zealand has to offer the best time to hike is from mid-October to April. It is summer during these months and the days are longer and the temperature is hotter. The best time to plan a great walk of New Zealand is in mid-November. During the great walk season heavy rain or snow can liner and the alpines can make it impossible with snow. Keep in mind when planning a trip with hiking that the weather can vary and be unpredictable no matter where you go in New Zealand. Be sure to plan alternative activities when planning your trip.


What To Pack

When Hiking in New Zealand, you should bring with you:

  • First Aid Kit- Just in case as you never know what may happen out on the trail.
  • Garbage Bag- All New Zealand national parks do not have garbage bins in them. So everything you bring in, you must also pack out. Don’t be a litter bug!
  • Hiking Boots- Its crutial to have hiking boots, especially for day trips. Weather varies in New Zealand and many trails can be snow covered, or muddy. To ensure your feet stay warm, dry, and comfortable bring your hiking boots.
  • Hiking Poles- On some trails, hiking poles can be very handy. It’s up to you if you’re comfortable hiking with them or not.
  • Insect Repellant- In parts of New Zealand, sand flies can be quite vicious. Insect repellant will be a massive life saver!
  • Knit Cap & Gloves- You will want a knit cap and gloves in your pack just in case the temperature drops. Always safety first on the trail.
  • Layers- No matter what time of the year you’re hiking in New Zealand, layers are necessary. The weather changes all the time. And, for those trails that gain altitude, it will get chilly. Wool layers help you stay warm or cool depending on the temperature. They also wick moisture away and prevents odors. A must when on the hiking trails of New Zealand.
  • Pocket Knife- You never know what you may encounter out on the trails, its important to come prepared for the worse.
  • Rain Jacket- You should always have a rain jacket in your pack even if it’s sunny when you start out.
  • Sleeping Bag- If you plan on camping, its a good idea to pack a sleeping bag. Even if you plan to stay in a hut along the trails its good to have a sleeping bag to stay warm.
  • Sun Screen- The sun in New Zealand is quite strong. Protect your skin by having sun screen, hat, head buff, and sun glasses.
  • Tent- Most trails offer huts or places to stay throughout the trails, though you have to book them months in advaces so its good to have a tent for an alternative option.
  • Toilet Paper- Though there are toilets on most of the hiking trails in New Zealand, not all have toilet paper. You’ll definitely want to have some in your pack with you.
  • Torch- For those early starts or trails with caves and tunnels, a torch is a must to have with you. I use this Black Diamond head torch and I love it, especially as you can control how bright the light is. And it seems to go on forever before you need to change out the batteries.
  • Water Bottle- Never start on a hiking trail in New Zealand without having water with you. And many trails don’t have a water source available. So, make sure you have plenty.
  • Waterproof Backpack(or protective outer liner)- You don’t want your belongings to get wet.
  • Whistle- The weather can change quickly in New Zealand. A whistle is a great item to have just in case of an emergency, especially if hiking solo. You’ll always find one in my pack.
  • Wool Socks- Not only are wool socks great for keeping your feet warm, they help regulate your temperature and keep the feet moisture free.
  • Additional- hats, sun glasses, lighters & matches, food/pots/utensils,

Plan your adventure

Okere Falls Trail, Rotorua, North Island

Explore forest walks that lead to a series of beautiful and breathtaking waterfalls, the Tutea Caves and Hinemoa’s Steps.Okere river, or also known as the ‘place of drifting’ plummets and scurries through a steep forested ravine before it enters the calm waters of the trout pool, which is a beautiful fishing spot where trout can be seen swimming against the current. Walking these trails in the reserve lead to four different and unique waterfalls, the fite of Rotorua’s first hydro-electric station, the Tutea Caves and Hinemoa’s steps. These steps were carved into the rock face in 1970 and lead to the waterfalls with some caves that were once used by local Maori women and children to hide from the war battles.

  • Distance: 3 kilometers / 1.9 miles
  • Start / Finish: Bay of Plenty
  • Time: 30 minutes to 1 hour
  • Elevation Change: 55 meters / 180 feet
  • Difficulty: Easy

Putangirua Pinnacles, Wellington, North Island

Walk to the spectacular Putangirua Pinnacles or further for excellent views of Palliser Bay and Lake Onoke – the shorter options are suitable for kids.Around 7 million years ago this range was an island but as the mountains eroded, scree and gravel were washed down to the coast where they formed a sedimentary layer. Over many years the Putangirua Stream exposed this layer of gravel to the erosive rain and many floods. Some of the sediments stayed together while others washed away which resulted in the Putangirua Pinnacles ‘an amazing collection of hoodoos’ This place was also the filming location for the third movie of the “Lord of the rings”. Despite this being a shorter trek it does not disappoint when it comes to views. There are three routes that lead to the Putangirua Pinnacles or the lookout and all start from the streambed. From this location you are able to take the loop track junction on the lookout or continue uphill for a longer walk to view the Palliser Bay and Lake Onoke.

  • Distance: 7 kilometers / 4.4 miles
  • Start / Finish: Putangirua Pinnacles Scenic Reserve
  • Time: 3 hours to half-day
  • Elevation Change: 279.8 meters / 918 feet
  • Difficulty: Easy

Lake Waikaremoana Great Walk, Hawke’s Bay, North Island

Lake Waikaremoana Great walk offers a 4 day guided tour. Join the group for a breathtaking journey that is not just a hike but also holds alot of rich history. Venture deep into the heart of Te Urewere and experience untouched, pristine forests and New Zealand's last frontier with land cloaked in mists. This walk offers everything. Just bring a day pack, for there will be a luggage boat and hut to hut sleeping day by day. Booking for the trip and huts must be made in advance.

  • Distance: 44 kilometers / 27.3 miles
  • Start / Finish: Onepoto to Hopuruahine
  • Time: 4 days
  • Elevation Change: 2194 meters / 7198 feet
  • Difficulty: Easy to Moderate

Rainbow Mountain & Kerosene Creek, Rotorua, North Island

This is ranked Top 10 best hikes on the north island, forming the letter ‘Y’. The trails start on the same track, and then divert to their respective destinations. We recommend doing Rainbow Mountain first, then hiking back to the fork in the road and making the trip up to Kerosene Creek to the best free hot spring in New Zealand. The hike up Rainbow Mountain is a bit of a challenge, as there are a few steep ascents on your way to the summit. There are a few wonderful lookouts including one over a red cliff, look closely to see steam rising from some geysers in the mountainside! On top of the Mountain on a clear day you can see for miles. If you are a mountain biker, there are mountain biking trails along the backside of the mountain, which take you right to Kerosene Creek! In the Summer you’ll see blackberry bushes lining the trail go ahead and have a little snack! The hot springs are an entire river, complete with flowing current and cascading waterfalls.

  • Distance: 6-9 kilometers / 3.7-5.6 miles
  • Start / Finish: off Thermal Explorer Highway
  • Time: 2.5 to 5 hours
  • Elevation Change: 285 meters / 930 feet
  • Difficulty: Moderate

Tongariro Alpine Crossing, Tongariro National Park, North Island

This is one of New Zealand’s Greatest Day Hikes. For any lord of the ring fans, Tongariro National Park is the land of Mordor. This hike weaves through rugged volcanic terrain and provides a spectacular views of Mount Ngauruhoe also known as Mount Doom, emerald pools, lakes and craters. This is a one way hike so you will need to plan ahead and organize a shuttle transport a few days in advance.

  • Distance: 19.4 kilometers / 12 miles
  • Start / Finish: Mangatepopo to Ketetahi
  • Time: 6 to 8 Hours
  • Elevation Change: 1125 meters / 3690 feet
  • Difficulty: Moderate

Rob Roy Glacier Track, Wanaka, South Island

This trail is family friendly and offers a variety of alpine landscape such as snowfields, glaciers, sheer rock cliffs, and waterfalls. Roy Rob Glacier includes a bridge that will take you across the West Matukituki River. The track climbs through a small gorge into beech forest, then into alpine vegetation at the head of the valley, and provides a spectacular view of the Rob Roy Glacier. No Dogs are allowed on this trail.

  • Distance: 10 kilometers / 6.2 miles
  • Start / Finish: Raspberry Creek Flat Car Park
  • Time: 3 to 4 hours
  • Elevation Change: 400 meters / 1312.3 feet
  • Difficulty: Easy

Hooker Valley Track, Aoraki Mount Cook National Park, South Island

This is one of the most popular hikes in Aoraki/Mt cook national park. Here you will pass through Hooker Valley and walk besides the Hooker River and back through the Southern Alps and Sealy Range. Mount cook is a rugged land of ice and rock with winding paths that seem endless. The tracks lead up to Hooker Valley along the hooker river ending at glacier lake where behind will be amazing views of Aoraki/Mount Cook buttercup on a clear or misty day. You might even catch a glimpse of an avalanche before they crash into the river. Along the way you will come across three unique swing bridges on top alpine streams and icebergs, glaciers and mountains. No Dogs are allowed on this trail.

  • Distance: 11 kilometers / 6.8 miles
  • Start / Finish: White Horse Hill Campground
  • Time: 3 hours
  • Elevation Change: 120 meters / 400 feet
  • Difficulty: Easy

Routeburn Track to Routeburn Falls, South Island

With soaring mountain peaks, huge endless valleys, waterfalls and crystal clear and calm lakes this track links the Mount Aspiring National Park with the Fiordland National Park. While this track might be shorter when it comes to multi-day hikes it does not lack diverse scenery. The highest point of the track is 1,255 meters above sea level, which in some places is unheard of. The part of New Zealand that the Routeburn track winds through has been shaped by glaciations into fiords, rocky coasts, cliffs, lakes and waterfalls. Bird watchers also love this place through the forest offer sections that house native tomtits, robins, fantails, wood pigeons and bellbirds and also the elusive alpine parrot. This is not a loop track and can only be walked in either direction, one track is at the end of Routeburn Shelter and the other is at the divide. It is recommended though to avoid this track between May-September when the risk of avalanches is high. The weather is also colder and daylight is shorter. Bookings are required for huts and alongside campsites.

  • Distance: 32 kilometers / 19.9 miles
  • Start / Finish: Routeburn Shelter at Lake Wakatipu to The Divide
  • Time: 2 to 4 Days
  • Elevation Change: 823 meters / 2700 feet
  • Difficulty: Easy to Moderate

Abel Tasman Coast Track, Abel Tasman National Park, South Island

This national park is perfect for hiking with over 30miles of golden sand beaches, granite cliffs, and lush subtropical rainforest around the park, that makes for a once in a lifetime hiking experience. Abel Tasman Coast Track is the least strenuous of the places to hike in New Zealand. This track takes you through verdant fern and manuka groves along empty shores, where you can camp at the many campsites within the park or stay in huts along the water. There are several inlet tides along this track such as the Awaroa Inlet which requires you to pass by at low tide, or you will get stuck if you time the tides wrong. It is highly recommended to plan ahead and set out early. In the evening you can enjoy watching the seals swim, spotting birds, and diving into the water.

  • Distance: 40 kilometers / 24.9 miles One Way
  • Start / Finish: Marahau to Totaranui
  • Time: 3 to 5 Days
  • Elevation Change: 1897.1 meters / 6224 feet
  • Difficulty: Moderate

Milford Track Great Walk, Fiordland National Park, South Island

The Milford Track is one of the great walks, or New Zealands premier hiking and walking tracks while passing through some of the most unique and awe-inspiring and pristine untouched landscapes. Experience the stunning rugged mountain beaks, breathtaking sheer drop canyons and majestic cascading waterfalls while you walk beside pristine rivers and amongst lush, green rainforests before reaching Milford sound. Between the months of late October to April this is known as the finest walk in the world. The tracks are easy to follow and well marked. Bookings are required for campsites and huts.

  • Distance: 53 kilometers / 33 miles
  • Start / Finish: Lake Te Anau at Glade Wharf to Sandfly Point
  • Time: 4 days
  • Elevation Change: 1755 meters / 5757.9 feet
  • Difficulty: Moderate

Mueller Hut Route, Aoraki Mount Cook National Park, South Island

The snow mountain range sets at 1800 meters on the sealy Range with a 360 range view of glaciers, ice cliffs, vertical rock faces and is known to house new Zealands highest peaks. You will start at Mount Cook Village already starting with amazing views of the Hooker Valley and the peaks of the area. After heading up a rock scramble around the ridge there is a hut to stay at but bookings are required. This hut has 28 bunk beds, cooking, lighting, mattresses and toilets. After the hut there is a three mile hike from the valley floor where it is steep and switchbacked. Despite the cliff, it does not take away from the immaculate view to the valleys braided river system or the bright blue glacial ponds that captures the mountains melt off. There are also seasonal restrictions for this track, only being opened Mid November-April.

  • Distance: 10.5 kilometers / 6.5 miles
  • Start / Finish: Mangatepopo to Ketetahi
  • Time: 7 hours or 2 days
  • Elevation Change: 1045 meters / 3425 feet
  • Difficulty: Moderate to Difficult

Dusky Track, Fiordland, South Island

The Dusty track can be walked in either direction, from Lake Hauroko to Lake Manapori. The trip is long but rewarding and easy to follow. There are 21 three wire hanging bridges to cross. Expect fallen trees, knee deep mud, river crossings, rock climbing and overall rough terrain. Some areas might become impassable due to flooding in certain months. Your first stop will be Lake Hauroko, which is the deepest lake in New Zealand at 462m and you will start out your trek with a boat ride across it. Enjoy the stunning views of Tamate/Dusty sound from the open tops of the Pleasant Range after the lake. Through the trip there are multiple huts and campsites to stay at that do require an ahead of time booking.

  • Distance: 84 kilometers / 52.2 miles
  • Start / Finish: Lake Hauroko to Lake Manapouri
  • Time: 7 to 8 days
  • Elevation Change: 1082 meters / 3550 feet
  • Difficulty: Challenging
